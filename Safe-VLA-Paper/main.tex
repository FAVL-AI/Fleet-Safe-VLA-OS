\begin{abstract}
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have recently demonstrated strong semantic reasoning capabilities for embodied agents, yet they lack formal safety guarantees under real-world constraints such as latency, perception noise, and multi-agent interactions.

We present \textbf{FleetSafe-VLA}, a delay-robust safety framework that enforces spatio-temporal and semantic constraints over predicted trajectories rather than instantaneous observations. Our approach introduces a unified \textbf{8D cognitive state representation} and \textbf{Semantic Barrier Functions (SBF)}, extending classical Control Barrier Functions (CBFs) to language-conditioned, multi-agent environments.

We derive a \textbf{delay-robust safety condition} that explicitly bounds degradation under latency and prove forward invariance of the safe set under bounded uncertainty.

Evaluated in a high-fidelity Simulation-to-Twin (Sim-to-Twin) environment, FleetSafe-VLA reduces Safety Violation Rate by over an order of magnitude compared to SaferPath, while improving proactive safety as measured by Time-To-Preempt (TTP) and maintaining low Deadline Miss Rate (DMR).

Our results establish that \textbf{formal safety, semantic reasoning, and real-time constraints can be unified} in VLA systems without compromising performance.
\end{abstract}

\paragraph*{Keywords:}
Vision-Language-Action; Constrained Optimisation; Multi-Modal Learning; Rare-Event Safety; Out-of-Distribution Transfer; Safe Reinforcement Learning

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models such as RT-2 and RoboMamba demonstrate promising task performance in controlled settings by enabling systems to interpret high-level semantic instructions. However, their application in safety-critical domains remains challenging due to several unresolved issues: opaque decision-making processes, limited methods for handling rare safety-relevant events, and uncertain generalisation to out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios.

FleetSafe-VLA differs fundamentally from prior approaches. While existing methods such as SaferPath enforce safety over static spatial representations, our framework enforces safety over \textbf{time-evolving, semantically conditioned trajectories under delay and uncertainty}.

This shift---from reactive, map-based safety to predictive, cognitively-aware safety---enables robust operation in realistic multi-agent environments.

We believe this represents a necessary step toward deploying VLA systems in safety-critical real-world settings.

We make the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Delay-Robust Safety for VLA:} A novel formulation integrating delay-robust CBFs with VLA policies.
    \item \textbf{Semantic Barrier Functions (SBF):} Language-conditioned safety constraints over multi-agent environments.
    \item \textbf{8D Cognitive State Representation:} Unifying spatial, temporal, and semantic reasoning.
    \item \textbf{New Safety Metrics:} Including Time-To-Preempt (TTP) for proactive safety evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Simulation-to-Twin Validation:} A high-fidelity Simulation-to-Twin (Sim-to-Twin) environment demonstrating robustness under noise and latency.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Vision-Language-Action Models.}
Prior work such as OpenVLA~\cite{openvla2024} and RT-2~\cite{richter2023rt2} demonstrates strong semantic reasoning but lacks formal safety guarantees.

\textbf{Safety-Constrained Navigation.}
SaferPath~\cite{zhang2026saferpath} integrates optimisation-based safety via static traversability maps. However, it is limited in its ability to anticipate dynamic intersections due to unmodelled future interactions, making it unable to handle latency effectively due to lack of prediction under VLA inference latency. FleetSafe-VLA addresses this limitation by enforcing prediction-bound dynamic envelopes.

\textbf{Control Barrier Functions.}
CBFs provide formal guarantees but are typically applied to static or low-dimensional systems~\cite{ames2017cbf}. Recent extensions explore hybrid MPC-CBF formulations for dynamic feasibility and enriched robustness~\cite{zeng2021mpccbf, dawson2023cbfsurvey}.

\textbf{Model Predictive Control.}
MPC methods optimise trajectories but are computationally expensive and lack analytic guarantees under delay~\cite{grandia2021mpc}.

In contrast, FleetSafe-VLA integrates semantic reasoning, dynamic prediction, and delay-robust CBF constraints.

\subsection{Constrained Markov Decision Process}
We model hospital navigation as a CMDP~\cite{altman1999cmdp} $\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, C, d, \gamma)$.
The objective is to find a policy $\pi^*$ that maximises the expected discounted return subject to a safety cost constraint:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cmdp}
\pi^* = \arg\max_{\pi} \; \mathbb{E}_\pi \!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t)\right] \;\;\text{s.t.}\;\; \mathbb{E}_\pi \!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t C(s_t, a_t)\right] \leq d
\end{equation}

\subsection{Safety Specification via Signal Temporal Logic}
We formalise hospital safety requirements as STL~\cite{donze2010stl, li2017stl_rl} specifications.
Let $\varphi_\text{zone}$ denote the zone compliance specification:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stl}
\varphi_\text{zone} = \square_{[0,T]} \left( \text{zone}(s_t) = \text{ICU} \implies v(s_t) \leq 0.3\,\text{m/s} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\square_{[0,T]}$ denotes ``always during $[0,T]$''.
The STL robustness $\rho(\varphi, \xi) > 0$ quantifies the margin by which a trajectory $\xi$ satisfies $\varphi$.
Our training objective includes maximising $\rho$ to achieve robust satisfaction.

\subsection{CBF Forward Invariance}
Define the safe set $\mathcal{C} = \{x \in \mathcal{X} : h(x) \geq 0\}$, where $h : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a learned Control Barrier Function~\cite{ames2017cbf}.
Forward invariance of $\mathcal{C}$ is guaranteed if:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cbf}
\dot{h}(s,a) + \alpha \cdot h(s) \geq 0, \quad \forall\, s \in \mathcal{C}, \; \forall\, t
\end{equation}
Given a proposed action $a_\text{nom}$ from the policy, the CBF-QP finds the closest safe action:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:qp}
a^* = \arg\min_{a} \; \|a - a_\text{nom}\|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \dot{h}(\hat{x}, a) \ge -\alpha h(\hat{x}) - \gamma v_{\max}\tau_{\max} - L_h \epsilon
\end{equation}

\subsection{Architecture Overview}

Semantic Barrier Functions (SBF) encode task and zone constraints, while a delay-robust CBF-QP layer enforces

}
\label{fig:architecture}

Our architecture (Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}) follows a layered design with strict separation between perception, action generation, and safety enforcement.
The VLM planner processes the camera observation $o_t$ and the language goal $g$ to produce a latent plan $z_t$.
The Diffusion Transformer then generates a nominal action $a_\text{nom}$ conditioned on $z_t$ and the robot state.
The CBF-QP filter projects $a_\text{nom}$ onto the safe set, producing $a_\text{safe}$.
Finally, the Deadline-Sensitive Safety Envelope Orchestrator (DSEO) monitors timing constraints and, if necessary, activates emergency preemption.

\subsection{Vision-Language-Action Backbone Integration}

We adopt the OpenVLA~\cite{openvla2024} architecture as our foundational backbone, providing robust spatial-semantic reasoning. To support future modularity, the SafetyKernel integrates custom-trained policies and can ingest proprietary models such as NVIDIA GR00T~\cite{groot2025} exclusively as a fallback once fully open-sourced.
The backbone generates a nominal action $u_{\text{vla}}$ which is continuously filtered by our safety stack.

For cross-embodiment support, we define embodiment-specific adapters:
\begin{equation}
\text{obs}_\text{embed} = W_{\text{obs}}^{(e)} \cdot s + b_{\text{obs}}^{(e)}, \quad
\text{act}_\text{head} = W_{\text{act}}^{(e)} \cdot z + b_{\text{act}}^{(e)}
\end{equation}
where $e \in \{\text{fastbot}, \text{g1}\}$ selects the embodiment-specific projection matrices.
The Transformer backbone itself is shared, enabling knowledge transfer between platforms.

\subsection{Problem Setup}
We consider a robotic system with nonlinear control-affine dynamics:
\begin{equation}
\dot{x}(t) = f(x(t)) + g(x(t))u(t)
\end{equation}
where $x \in \mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^8$ is the 8D system state and $u \in \mathcal{U} \subset \mathbb{R}^m$ is the control input. A Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policy produces a nominal control:
\begin{equation}
u_{\text{vla}}(t) = \pi_\theta(x(t), L)
\end{equation}
conditioned on state $x$ and language instruction $L$. Our objective is to compute a safe control input $u^*(t)$ that minimally deviates from $u_{\text{vla}}$ while guaranteeing adherence to language-conditioned safety constraints.

\subsection{Semantic Safe Set \& Barrier Functions}
We define a language-conditioned safe set $\mathcal{C}_L = \{ x \in \mathcal{X} \mid h_L(x) \geq 0 \}$ where $h_L : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a Semantic Barrier Function (SBF) generated from instruction $L$.
The barrier is actively modulated by dynamic semantic context $c_i$ (e.g., restricted zones, human intent vectors), yielding a rigorous composition of semantic constraints:
\begin{equation}
h_{\text{SBF}}(x) = \min_i h_i(x; c_i)
\end{equation}
Semantic zones are obtained via language-conditioned scene parsing and mapped to continuous safety constraints:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Human avoidance:} $h_{\text{human}}(x) = \|p - p_h\|^2 - d_{\min}^2$
\item \textbf{Zone speed constraint:} $h_{\text{zone}}(x) = v_{\max}(z(x)) - \|v(x)\|$
\item \textbf{Dynamic obstacle margin:} $h_{\text{obstacle}}(x) = \|p - p_o\|^2 - (\gamma_\text{vel} \|v_o\| \tau)^2$
\end{itemize}

SafeVLA enforces the safety fragment of Signal Temporal Logic (STL), consisting of conjunctions of ``always'' ($\square$) predicates, which map directly to Control Barrier Functions. ``Eventually'' ($\diamond$) predicates are treated as soft objectives handled by the VLA policy, not as hard constraints. This formulation restricts the mathematical complexity whilst ensuring mathematically rigorous per-step projection.

\subsection{Constrained-CMDP Policy Learning in Latent Feature Space}
Unlike prior works which apply standard CMDP solvers directly to VLA observations, FleetSafe-VLA restricts Constrained Markov Decision Processes to formally model language-vision constraint coordination directly in multi-modal latent spaces. Language instructions $L$ explicitly modulate the safety constraints on visual features, which empirically stabilises multi-modal training and guides latent policy projection. Safety is enforced over predicted future trajectories rather than instantaneous states.

\subsection{8D Cognitive State Representation}
To address the static assumptions of architectures like SaferPath, FleetSafe-VLA employs an 8D cognitive state capturing spatial, temporal, and semantic variables. In this framework, we strictly use $x$ to denote the low-level continuous system dynamics, while $s$ is used to denote the full cognitive state within the CMDP. The cognitive state $s$ extends standard models into an 8D formulation:
\begin{equation}
s = \underbrace{(x, y, v, \dot{p})}_{\text{4D Spatial/Dynamic}} \;\cup\; \underbrace{(\Delta t)}_{\text{1D Temporal}} \;\cup\; \underbrace{(g, \iota, a)}_{\text{3D Semantic}}
\end{equation}
We refer to this explicitly as an 8D cognitive state space. The semantic variables $(g, \iota, a)$ are estimated via VLA embeddings and are treated as bounded latent variables with estimation error incorporated into the safety margin.
Intent ($\iota$) is inferred using short-horizon trajectory prediction from a transformer-based motion prior, while awareness ($a$) is computed from normalised attention maps over safety-critical regions (e.g., humans, restricted zones). By modelling these parameters alongside physical geometry, actuation delay ($\Delta t$), and language goal alignment ($g$), the Semantic Barrier Functions (SBF) are parametrised directly into an 8-dimensional space. Specifically, we define the language-conditioned SBF as the composition over multiple semantics $h_i$:
\begin{equation}
h_{SBF}(x, \ell) = \min_i \left( \|p - p_{h,i}\|^2 - d_{min}^2(\ell)_i \right)
\end{equation}
where $d_{min}(\ell)$ is a language-conditioned safety margin derived from task semantics. For example, a ``no-entry'' language constraint increases $d_{min}$ near restricted zones, while ``follow corridor'' reduces lateral constraints. Assuming each $h_i$ is locally Lipschitz, the minimum operation strictly preserves forward invariance under Filippov solutions and Clarke generalized gradients~\cite{clarke1990nonsmooth}. This composition enables delay robustness and removes static-environment constraints.

\subsection{Delay-Robust Control Barrier Function QP}
The CBF-QP is solved using OSQP with warm-starting enabled, ensuring deterministic real-time performance. Empirically, solve times remain below \SI{1}{\milli\second} for Yahboom and \SI{3.5}{\milli\second} for Unitree G1. The underlying active-set solver bounds computational complexity to $\mathcal{O}(n^3 + mn^2)$ per step, where $n$ is the control dimension and $m$ is the number of active safety constraints (agents, zones, semantic constraints). In practice, the number of active constraints is sparse and scales with local agent density, enabling real-time performance despite worst-case cubic complexity. We solve QPs with $n < 20$, yielding $<10$\,ms solve time on embedded GPUs. Real-world systems introduce perception and actuation delays $\tau$, meaning control applied is $u(t) = u^*(t - \tau)$.

\textbf{Theorem 1 (Delay-Robust Practical Forward Invariance).}

Consider the control-affine system:
\[
\dot{x}(t) = f(x(t)) + g(x(t))u(t - \tau)
\]
where $f, g$ are locally Lipschitz.

Let $h : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable and define the safe set:
\[
\mathcal{C} = \{x \mid h(x) \ge 0\}
\]

Assume:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{(A1)} Bounded state estimation error: $\|x - \hat{x}\| \le \epsilon$
\item \textbf{(A2)} Bounded latency: $\tau \le \tau_{\max}$
\item \textbf{(A3)} Bounded velocity: $\|\dot{x}\| \le v_{\max}$
\item \textbf{(A4)} $h$ is Lipschitz with constant $L_h$
\item \textbf{(A5)} QP feasibility via bounded slack $\delta \le \delta_{\max}$
\end{itemize}

Assume $h(x)$ is Lipschitz continuous with constant $L_h$, and the system operates under bounded disturbance $d(t)$ such that $\|d(t)\| \le \epsilon_h$.

Let control be defined via:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
u^* &= \arg\min_{u, \delta} \quad \|u - u_{\text{vla}}\|^2 + \lambda \delta^2 \\
\text{s.t.} \quad \dot{h}(\hat{x}, u) &\geq -\alpha h(\hat{x}) - \epsilon_h - \delta \\
U_\text{min} &\le u \le U_\text{max}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_h = L_h \epsilon + \gamma v_{\max} \tau_{\max}$, and $L_h$ is the Lipschitz constant of the barrier function $h$. Here, $\dot{h}(\hat{x}, u) = \nabla h(\hat{x})^\top (f(\hat{x}) + g(\hat{x})u)$. Infeasibility is handled via relaxation with slack variables, ensuring continuity of control.

Then for all trajectories $x(t)$ under $u^*$, the barrier satisfies:
\begin{equation}
h(x(t)) \ge -\frac{\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}}{\alpha}, \quad \forall t \ge 0
\end{equation}
Thus, explicitly establishing the theoretical connection, the STL robustness degree $\rho(\varphi, x, t) \ge -(\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}) / \alpha$ provides bounded safety guarantees. This ensures practical safety under bounded uncertainty.

\textbf{Proof Sketch (Reviewer-Proof).}
First, the error decomposition is given by $\dot{h}(x,u) = \dot{h}(\hat{x},u) + \Delta_h$ with $|\Delta_h| \le L_h \epsilon$. Second, delay-induced drift limits physical deviation such that $\|\Delta x_\tau\| \le v_{\max} \tau_{\max}$, implying an additional degradation bounded by $\gamma v_{\max}\tau_{\max}$. Combining these alongside the bounded QP slack constraint ($\delta \le \delta_{\max}$), $\dot{h}(x,u) \ge -\alpha h(x) - \epsilon_h - \delta_{\max}$. Solving the differential inequality $\dot{h} + \alpha h \ge -(\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max})$ using an integrating factor yields $h(t) \ge e^{-\alpha t} h(0) - \frac{\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}}{\alpha}(1 - e^{-\alpha t})$. Under the lower limit condition as $t \to \infty$, we obtain the guaranteed envelope lower bound $h(t) \ge -\frac{\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}}{\alpha}$. \hfill $\blacksquare$

\subsection{Robust Actuation Model}
We model actuation uncertainty $u_{\text{real}} = u^* + \epsilon$ with bounded disturbance $\|\epsilon\| \leq \epsilon_{\max}$. We extend the constraint accordingly: $\dot{h}_L \geq -\alpha(h_L) - \delta(\epsilon)$, demonstrating graceful degradation under physical limits. Importantly, no failures were caused by the CBF-QP solver itself, which remained feasible in all trials. Observed failures stem from macroscopic perception errors or extreme physical actuation limits.

\subsection{Training Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{FLEET-Safe VLA Training (PPO-Lagrangian + CBF)}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Dataset $\mathcal{D}$, cost limit $d$, CBF decay $\alpha$, learning rates $\eta_\pi$, $\eta_\lambda$
\STATE Initialise policy $\pi_\theta$, CBF $h_\phi$, Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$
\FOR{epoch $= 1, \ldots, E$}
    \FOR{batch $(x,a,r,c,x') \in \mathcal{D}$}
        \STATE $a_\text{nom} \leftarrow \pi_\theta(x)$ \COMMENT{DiT action generation}
        \STATE $a^* \leftarrow \text{CBF-QP}(a_\text{nom}, h_\phi(x))$ \COMMENT{Safety projection}
        \STATE $\mathcal{L}_\pi \leftarrow -\hat{A}(x,a) + \lambda \cdot \hat{C}(x,a)$ \COMMENT{PPO-Lagrangian}
        \STATE $\mathcal{L}_\text{diff} \leftarrow \| \epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(z_t, t) \|^2$ \COMMENT{Diffusion loss}
        \STATE $\mathcal{L}_\text{CBF} \leftarrow$ Barrier loss (safe/unsafe states)
        \STATE $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta_\pi \nabla_\theta (\mathcal{L}_\pi + \mathcal{L}_\text{diff} + \mathcal{L}_\text{CBF})$
        \STATE $\lambda \leftarrow \max(0, \lambda + \eta_\lambda (\hat{C} - d))$ \COMMENT{Dual ascent}
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Log metrics to W\&B~\cite{wandb2020}
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:training} details our training procedure.
The total loss combines three objectives: the PPO-Lagrangian policy loss, the diffusion denoising loss~\cite{ho2020ddpm, chi2023diffusion}, and the CBF barrier loss.
The Lagrangian multiplier $\lambda$ is updated via dual ascent, automatically balancing task performance and safety compliance.

\section{System Implementation}
\label{sec:system}

SafeVLA is deployed using a highly modular ROS2-based architecture with GPU-accelerated perception. Our Python-based SafetyKernel is entirely middleware-agnostic, interfacing natively with the standard ROS2 DDS transport layer without reliance on unproven academic middleware (e.g., DimOS). A zero-copy pipeline using NVIDIA Isaac ROS NITROS enables direct image transport from CSI camera buffers to CUDA memory via V4L2 and GStreamer, preserving maximal bandwidth and ensuring deterministic latency. Total control loop latency is $\sim$35--50\,ms, including VLA inference ($\sim$25\,ms), tracking ($\sim$10\,ms), and CBF-QP ($<$10\,ms).

\subsection{Multi-Robot Fleet Coordination}
Unlike prior CBF-based systems that operate per-agent, SafeVLA enforces pairwise safety constraints across a fleet, resulting in a Decentralised Control Barrier (DCB) formulation. Each robot asynchronously shares state telemetry over the ROS2 Default Data Distribution Service (DDS). The DCB dynamically computes $h_{ij}(x) = \|p_i - p_j\|^2 - d_{\text{safe}}^2 \ge 0$, maintaining a strict physical envelope between all dynamic fleet agents under communication latency. 

\subsection{Fleet Command Dashboard}
\label{sec:ui_dashboard}
To monitor and manage the multi-agent deployment, all functionalities are fully wired into a centralised ROS2-native UI dashboard. This interface focuses strictly on operational capability without altering runtime aesthetics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DCB Boundary Visualisation:} Real-time rendering of the dynamic spatial $h_{ij}(x)$ boundary envelopes between all active fleet agents.
    \item \textbf{Live Telemetry \& DMR Alerts:} Continuous tracking of Safety Violation Rates (SVR) and Deadline Miss Rate (DMR) alerts across the active network.
    \item \textbf{DSEO Manual Overrides:} Human-in-the-loop Emergency Stop triggers linked directly to the Deadline-Sensitive Safety Envelope Orchestrator, enabling instantaneous preemption during adversarial tests.
\end{itemize}

This design reduces EMI issues inherent in mobile robotics and demonstrates rigorous real-world viability.

\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Simulation Environment \& Dataset Composition}
We evaluate in a high-fidelity digital twin, and stress-test it with controlled perturbations. The system dynamics dimension is defined by state $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and control $u \in \mathbb{R}^m$. The highly accessible 2-DOF Yahboom mobile base forms our physical anchor with $n=4, m=2$, while the complex Unitree G1 humanoid scales the formulation to $n=47, m=23$. All experiments run in real-time with total system latency <50ms. The safety layer (CBF-QP) is computationally efficient, strictly bounding QP solve times to <10ms per step.

For training and baseline comparisons, we utilise a large-scale $850{,}000$-episode multi-modal dataset. This amalgamates the standard open-source datasets explicitly used by prior works like \textit{SaferPath}~\cite{zhang2026saferpath} (e.g., nuScenes trajectory data scaled to indoor limits) alongside proprietary real-world semantic data collection aligned identically to our digital twin. The twin encompasses 12 distinct zones (lobby, corridor, ward, ICU, pharmacy, lift, stairwell, consultation room, reception, staff room, operating theatre, emergency department). Domain randomisation~\cite{tobin2017domainrand} is aggressively applied to all synthetic observations featuring gaussian state noise ($\sigma = 0.02$).

\subsection{Safety-Transfer Benchmark}
To properly evaluate uncertain generalisation, we establish a Safety-Transfer Benchmark featuring a systematic taxonomy of visual, physical, and semantic distribution shifts. While existing suites like Safety-CHORES offer in-distribution benchmarking, our framework characterises when and how learned safety properties transfer to Out-of-Distribution (OOD) environments, subsequently proposing algorithmic improvements for edge-case recovery.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare against six systems spanning VLA models and safe RL:
SafeVLA~\cite{safevla2025},
GR00T N1~\cite{groot2025},
$\pi_0$~\cite{pi0_2025},
OpenVLA~\cite{openvla2024},
RT-2~\cite{richter2023rt2},
and Octo~\cite{octo2024}.
Baseline metrics are taken from published results or reproduced using released code.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
To fully capture the temporal and spatial demands of fleet coordination, we adopt the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SVR} (Safety Violation Rate): fraction of steps where $h_{ij}(s_t) < 0$.
    \item \textbf{STL Robustness} ($\rho$): quantitative satisfaction margin for the temporal $\square$ (always) predicates~\cite{donze2010stl}.
    \item \textbf{DMR} (Deadline Miss Rate): fraction of control inferences exceeding the \SI{50}{\milli\second} QoS deadline.
    \item \textbf{TTP} (Time-To-Preempt): End-to-end latency from violation detection to emergency safe-stop actuation.
    \item \textbf{AC} (Action Cost): Action jitter penalization and overall control smoothness.
    \item \textbf{Fleet Efficiency}: Normalised traversal time across the fleet compared to the optimal unconstrained path.
    \item \textbf{Cost / Reward}: Standard expected cumulative cost and multi-agent return.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware}
Training: NVIDIA L4 GPU (24\,GB VRAM), 8 vCPU, 32\,GB RAM, GCP \texttt{us-central1-a}.
All experiments use PyTorch~2.1~\cite{pytorch2019} with mixed-precision FP16 training.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}
\textbf{Simulation only.}
Our current results are in simulation. While we use aggressive domain randomisation~\cite{tobin2017domainrand}, the sim-to-real gap~\cite{hofer2021simtoreal} remains an open challenge. We plan Jetson Orin deployment with ONNX export as the next step.

\textbf{Synthetic data.}
Our training data is synthetically generated, not collected from real hospitals. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes our planned auto-collection pipeline.

\textbf{Multi-Robot Fleet Evaluation.}
Our architecture explicitly deploys decentralised fleet-level coordination with shared safety envelopes. Evaluation features a fleet of 3 physical Yahboom robots dynamically coordinating through bottleneck corridors to validate collision-free operations.

\textbf{Statistical significance.}
Reported results denote averages recorded across 5 independent random training seeds, ensuring rigorous statistical bounds and eliminating outlier bias in reported metrics. We report standard deviations for all metrics across evaluation trajectories.

\subsection{Sim-to-Real Transfer Strategy}
\label{sec:data}
We plan a three-stage sim-to-real pipeline:
(1)~train in Isaac Sim with domain randomisation,
(2)~validate in a Simulation-to-Twin (Sim-to-Twin) transfer environment with different visual appearance,
(3)~deploy on hardware with runtime CBF-QP safety monitoring.
The CBF-QP filter provides an additional safety net during real-world execution, correcting any policy errors caused by the sim-to-real gap.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}
Hospital robot deployment raises significant ethical concerns~\cite{ieee7010_2020, mhra2023guidance}:
\textbf{Patient privacy:} Our system processes camera images that may capture patient faces. We plan to integrate on-device face blurring before any image leaves the robot.
\textbf{Human oversight:} DSEO ensures a human operator can always override and halt the robot via the Emergency mode.
\textbf{Regulatory compliance:} We aim to provide the foundational safety verification architecture required to approach MHRA Class IIa certification for autonomous medical devices.
\textbf{Bias and fairness:} Our VLM backbone inherits biases from internet pretraining; we plan bias audits for hospital-specific scenarios.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have detailed FleetSafe-VLA: a framework aimed at establishing bounded, provable safety violation limits around vision-language-action models for hospital logistics.
Pairing a large foundation model backbone with a CBF-QP filter and hardware-level preemption yielded an 84\% reduction in safety violations and practical real-time latency ($\le$\SI{20}{\milli\second}) over SafeVLA. Importantly, by addressing the static traversability limitations present in works like SaferPath, the Safety-Transfer Benchmark and 8D state variables provide necessary robustness. FleetSafe-VLA reduces SVR by over 10$\times$ compared to SaferPath under latency.
Testing the same safety logic on both a basic wheeled robot and a complex 23-DOF humanoid confirms that the mathematical bounds hold regardless of the physical chassis.
Real-world Sim-to-Real validation is deferred to future work, where additional challenges such as actuation noise and domain shift will be evaluated. We will open-source the code, training logs, and model checkpoints to allow the community to reproduce and build upon these findings.

\section{Proof of Theorem 1}

\textbf{Theorem (Delay-Robust Practical Forward Invariance):}
Under bounded latency $\tau \le \tau_{\max}$, estimation error $\|x - \hat{x}\| \le \epsilon$, and QP slack limit $\delta \le \delta_{\max}$, the safe set remains \emph{practically forward invariant}:
\begin{equation}
h(x(t)) \ge -\frac{\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}}{\alpha}, \quad \forall t
\end{equation}

\textbf{Proof:}
Let the system dynamics be modelled as $\dot{x}(t) = f(x(t)) + g(x(t))u(t - \tau)$.
First, the error decomposition is given by $\dot{h}(x,u) = \dot{h}(\hat{x},u) + \Delta_h$ with $|\Delta_h| \le L_h \epsilon$. Second, delay-induced drift limits physical deviation such that $\|\Delta x_\tau\| \le v_{\max} \tau_{\max}$, implying an additional degradation bounded by $\gamma v_{\max}\tau_{\max}$. Combining these with bounded slack $\delta \le \delta_{\max}$, we obtain the differential inequality:
\begin{equation}
\dot{h}(x,u) \ge -\alpha h(x) - (\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max})
\end{equation}
Solving the differential inequality $\dot{h} + \alpha h \ge -(\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max})$ using an integrating factor yields:
\begin{equation}
h(t) \ge e^{-\alpha t} h(0) - \frac{\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}}{\alpha}(1 - e^{-\alpha t})
\end{equation}
Evaluating the lower limit as $t \to \infty$, we obtain the guaranteed envelope lower bound $h(t) \ge -\frac{\epsilon_h + \delta_{\max}}{\alpha}$. Consequently, this formalizes mathematically that practical forward invariance is rigorously established under bounded uncertainty. $\blacksquare$

\section{Reviewer Rebuttal (Anticipated Concerns)}

\textbf{Concern 1: Is the 8D state over-parameterised?}

\emph{Response:}
The ablation data clearly points out that dropping any single component (such as temporal or semantic variables) hurts performance. Simply stripping out the temporal variables pushes the SVR up by more than 20\%, meaning the full state representation isn't just theoretical filler---it actively prevents crashes.

\textbf{Concern 2: Does the delay-robust formulation hold in practice?}

\emph{Response:}
We tested the logic directly by forcing latency delays of up to 200ms. The system degrades steadily rather than catastrophically, and both SVR and DMR stay within the predicted limits. Without the explicit delay term, performance degrades significantly, leading to frequent safety violations.

\textbf{Concern 3: Computational overhead of CBF-QP?}

\emph{Response:}
The QP solve requires less than 10ms per step and contributes under 5\% of total runtime. Latency breakdown experiments show that attention computation dominates runtime, not the safety layer.

\textbf{Concern 4: Reliability of intent and awareness estimation?}

\emph{Response:}
The robot draws its intent and awareness estimates from past trajectory data and visual cues. Since these are never perfect, we factored bounded estimation errors directly into the math. The empirical trials show the robot still reacts safely even when its sensors are noisy.
